Nirmal Kumar Rana Associate Professor school Ph.D.: Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur email nirmalrana[at]iitj[dot]ac[dot]in biotech Asymmetric Synthesis & Continuous Flow Chemistry call 0291 280 1311 language Web Profile Biography Dr. Rana obtained his Ph.D. from the
Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur (IITK), India, in 2012 under the supervision
of Prof. Vinod K. Singh. Then, he moved to the research group of Prof. John
C.-G. Zhao at the University of Texas at San Antonio (UTSA), USA. After two
years post-doctoral research at UTSA, he returned to India, and worked as a
senior research scientist in the Department of Chemistry, IITK for one year.
Then, in 2015, he moved to the Indian Institute of Science Education and
Research Bhopal as an INSPIRE Faculty Fellow. In November 2017, he joined as a
faculty in the Department of Chemistry, Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur,
India. He is currently serving as an Associate Professor in the Department of
Chemistry, IIT Jodhpur. He is recipient of many prestigious awards and
recognitions, such as, First Prize of Eli Lilly Thesis Awards in 2012, DST-INSPIRE
Faculty Award in 2015. He has recently been awarded with Chirantan Rasayan
Sanstha (CRS) Young Scientist Medal in 2024. He was a Review Editor of the
Editorial Board of Catalytic Reactions and Chemistry from 2021 to 2024. Research His research interest includes to
design and synthesize new organocatalysts and apply them to develop novel
asymmetric methodologies, mainly emphasizing domino/cascade/sequential
reactions to create multiple stereocenters within a molecule. Additional
targets are the exploration of dual organo-metal catalysis and bio-catalysis. His
research group has been actively working on the development of novel
methodologies using continuous-flow systems with immobilized catalysts for
producing fine chemicals and chiral drug molecules/intermediates with
industrial implementation. The developed protocols are utilized as key steps
for the synthesis of architecturally interesting and biologically active
molecules.
